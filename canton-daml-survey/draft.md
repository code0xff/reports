# Canton and Daml — detailed survey with emphasis on builder and developer perspectives

## Abstract

Canton is a privacy-preserving blockchain protocol whose smart contracts are written in Daml, a purely functional, Haskell-derived domain-specific language originally built and open-sourced by Digital Asset [^s01][^s19][^s20][^s21]. This report surveys Canton and Daml from two angles: the **developer perspective** — how application logic is written, tested, integrated, and upgraded — and the **builder perspective** — how a team chooses between private, consortium, and public Canton Network deployments, onboards to the Global Synchronizer, and reasons about cost, licensing, and governance [^s03][^s28][^s30][^s31][^s37]. Notable production references include Goldman Sachs' GS DAP, Deutsche Börse / Clearstream's D7, HKEX Synapse, and Broadridge's Distributed Ledger Repo platform [^s11][^s13][^s15][^s17]. The Canton Network mainnet went live in 2024 [^s02] and Canton Coin (CC) is its native utility token [^s06][^s07][^s08]. Several quantitative claims remain vendor-stated, and peer-reviewed analysis is limited to the Daml language paper rather than the Canton protocol itself [^s35] — those limitations are called out explicitly.

## 1. Introduction

Canton sits in a small but visibly active segment of enterprise distributed-ledger technology: protocols designed from the start to preserve data-partitioning, party-level privacy, and regulatory compliance rather than the "everyone sees everything" model of public EVM chains [^s04]. Digital Asset announced the Canton Network publicly in May 2023 with about thirty founding institutions [^s01], and the public Global Synchronizer — the BFT-ordered interoperability service at the centre of the Canton Network — went live on MainNet in June/July 2024 alongside Canton Coin [^s02][^s03]. Governance of that public deployment is run through the Global Synchronizer Foundation, hosted under the Linux Foundation umbrella rather than by Digital Asset directly [^s03][^s36].

The builder/developer split matters because different audiences interact with different parts of the stack. A developer writing a capital-markets workflow typically sees Daml — the language, the Ledger API, the testing tooling, the Daml Finance library [^s10][^s22][^s23][^s25][^s09]. A builder standing up a production deployment sees Canton the protocol: topology choices, synchronizer operation, licensing, and — for public-network deployments — the Canton Foundation's onboarding process [^s28][^s30][^s31][^s37]. This report tries to be explicit about which layer each claim applies to.

## 2. Background — Origins, Licensing, and Ecosystem

Daml predates the Canton protocol. Digital Asset released the Daml SDK under the Apache 2.0 license on 4 April 2019, after a private beta in 2018, making the language, runtime, and SDK freely usable by third parties [^s19]. The `digital-asset/daml` repository on GitHub is still Apache-2.0 and is written primarily in Haskell (≈66% of the codebase), with an `sdk/` subtree that hosts the compiler and SDK tooling [^s20]. Canton, the ledger-interoperability protocol that synchronises Daml participants, is hosted at `digital-asset/canton`, also Apache-2.0, written almost entirely in Scala, and described in its own README as "a next-generation Daml ledger interoperability protocol that implements Daml's built-in models of authorization and privacy faithfully" [^s21]. The reference implementation plus its 138+ releases live there [^s21].

Not every part of the stack is permissively licensed, however. Digital Asset offers a separate **Enterprise Suite for Canton** — an enterprise distribution with validator-node features, integration components such as PQS and Shell, composable "DA Utilities" modules, and additional infrastructure for private subnets [^s31]. Enterprise releases are available on request and not shipped under the Apache license that covers the public protocol repos [^s31]. Splice, the Hyperledger Labs project that powers the Global Synchronizer itself and includes the Amulet reference payment app (Canton Coin), is Apache-2.0 and community-maintained but was originally developed by Digital Asset [^s28][^s29]. The practical consequence is that "Canton and Daml are open source" is a true but incomplete statement — parts of the stack that matter in production are under a commercial licence _(interpretive)_ [^s31][^s32].

Production adoption has come from regulated venues rather than consumer applications. Goldman Sachs' tokenisation platform GS DAP™ went live in January 2023, with the European Investment Bank's EUR 100M digital bond as its inaugural issuance, and the Canton Network ecosystem page confirms GS DAP is deployed natively on Canton [^s11][^s12]. Deutsche Börse and Clearstream's D7 post-trade platform is built on Daml; Ledger Insights reported over 250,000 digital issuances processed by D7 by 2023 [^s13][^s14]. HKEX launched Synapse, a DAML-backed settlement platform for Northbound Stock Connect, in October 2023 [^s15][^s16]. Broadridge's Distributed Ledger Repo (DLR) platform, built on Daml and VMware Blockchain, went into production in mid-2021 [^s18] and Broadridge press releases reported US$385B in average daily processed repo trade volumes in October 2025 and US$368B in November 2025 (US$7.4 trillion monthly) [^s38][^s17]. These are trade-volume figures, not protocol-throughput benchmarks, but they demonstrate that Daml is sustaining real institutional workflows rather than sitting in pilot pipelines.

## 3. Architecture and Privacy Model

Canton is a "network of networks" [^s21][^s33]. A Canton deployment is made up of **participant nodes** — which hold Daml contract state on behalf of one or more on-ledger parties — and **synchronizers** (called "sync domains" in older docs), which distribute encrypted messages, order transactions, and confirm commits among a set of participants [^s21]. A single Canton installation can connect to multiple synchronizers and contracts can move between them, which is the mechanism Canton uses for composability across subnets without an external bridge [^s33].

The distinguishing feature is the privacy model. Canton implements **need-to-know, sub-transaction privacy**: participants receive and record only the parts of a transaction that apply to them, and synchronizer operators cannot see transaction payloads at all — they only see metadata such as ordering and participant identifiers [^s04]. A delivery-vs-payment settlement, for example, lets a cash bank see only the payment leg while a securities registrar sees only the asset leg [^s04]. The cryptographic construction underlying this is specified in the Canton whitepaper [^s05] _(unverified — single primary source; whitepaper is vendor-authored)_. The Daml language paper, the only peer-reviewed artefact in this space, formalises the abstract ledger over which Daml is evaluated but does not itself measure or externally audit Canton's implementation [^s35].

The public Canton Network adds one more layer: the **Global Synchronizer**, a decentralised BFT synchronizer run by multiple independent **Super Validators** that reaches consensus by a 2/3 BFT quorum [^s03][^s28]. Super Validators never see raw transaction data — they only order encrypted blobs — which is the same sub-transaction privacy guarantee extended to the public backbone [^s04]. Canton Coin (CC) is the utility token used to pay application and infrastructure fees on the Global Synchronizer and to reward Super Validators and application validators in exchange for that BFT operation [^s06][^s07][^s08]. Rewards are emitted in ten-minute "round" cycles, and the reward split between Super Validators, application providers, and end-user validators evolves over time — Digital Asset's own tokenomics docs currently describe a trajectory toward 62% applications / 20% Super Validators through mid-2029 [^s08]. CC launched with no pre-mine, no pre-sale, and no founder/VC allocation per the project's own statement, which if accurate is unusual for an institutional L1 _(vendor-stated)_ [^s07].

## 4. Developer Perspective — Writing Daml on Canton

Daml is purely functional and Haskell-derived [^s35]. The core authoring unit is a **template**, which declares the data of a contract, its **signatories** (parties whose authorisation is required for its creation), **observers** (parties allowed to see it), and a list of named **choices** — each choice is an action on the contract, gated by an explicitly listed **controller** party or party set [^s22]. Authorisation is enforced at the language level: if the combined parties listed as controller are not authorised to exercise the choice, the compiler and runtime reject it, rather than relying on ad hoc `require(msg.sender == …)` statements as typical EVM smart contracts do [^s22][^s35]. The language's arXiv paper frames this as providing "concepts to capture the legal rules that govern real-world business workflows" with a built-in authorisation framework that supports delegation and composition [^s35].

Applications interact with Canton through two parallel Ledger APIs. The **gRPC Ledger API** is the low-level protocol for streaming transactions, submitting commands, and querying active contracts; its `.proto` definitions are shipped with the SDK [^s23]. The **HTTP JSON API** is a simpler REST-like surface intended for web apps and scripting, and is the recommended path for TypeScript / JavaScript frontends [^s24]. The `daml` assistant ships code generators that convert the DAML-LF binary representation of contracts into idiomatic TypeScript or Java bindings, so integration code does not have to be written against the raw wire format [^s23]. These bindings plus Daml React libraries mean a builder typically writes application code in Daml and glue code in TypeScript or Java on the JVM.

Testing relies on **Daml Script**, which lets a developer script multi-party workflows as code and run them against an IDE sandbox, the CLI, a local Sandbox ledger, or any real Ledger API endpoint [^s25][^s26]. Scripts substitute for `hardhat`-style off-chain testing: they can create contracts, exercise choices on behalf of different parties, and assert the resulting ledger state [^s25]. Long-running automation — reacting to events on the ledger — uses **Daml Triggers**, a sibling abstraction that runs continuously against a Ledger API endpoint; Triggers are referenced in the SDK docs alongside Script but are a separate tool [^s26].

For capital-markets developers, the **Daml Finance** library is a material productivity lever. It is a first-party, open-source Daml library that provides reusable templates for instruments, accounts, settlement, and lifecycle events [^s09][^s10]. Daml Finance does not replace application code; it gives a common, composable vocabulary for assets that can be re-used across Canton applications and thus across the Canton Network [^s10][^s14].

Daml's upgrade story is a genuine differentiator from the EVM model. **Smart Contract Upgrade (SCU)** lets the author publish a new version of a Daml package while existing contracts stay valid; compatible changes are picked up without migrating each contract manually, and breaking changes are expressed by publishing a new package name [^s27]. SCU requires DAML-LF version 1.17 and Canton Protocol version 7 at minimum, and runtime package resolution is deterministic — the (package-name, package-version) map must be unique [^s27]. This is a documented capability; real-world operational experience across the larger Canton Network remains an open question _(early signal)_ [^s27].

## 5. Builder Perspective — Deploying, Running, and Joining the Canton Network

A builder choosing Canton has at least three topological options. **Fully private Canton** — one operator running Daml participant nodes and a single synchronizer — is the topology behind platforms such as GS DAP, D7, HKEX Synapse, and Broadridge DLR, where a regulated venue wants Daml's authorisation and privacy model but does not need to share state across institutional boundaries [^s11][^s13][^s15][^s17][^s37]. **Consortium Canton** — multiple institutions running participant nodes against a shared synchronizer operated by one of them or a neutral party — is the next step up and was historically how the Canton Network was piloted before the Global Synchronizer existed [^s01][^s37]. **Public Canton Network** — joining the Global Synchronizer via a participant node — is the newest topology, opened with the 2024 MainNet launch [^s02][^s03]. The "5 developer pathways" framing from the Canton Network team spells out this spectrum explicitly and maps to what builders actually face in practice [^s37].

Operating on the public Canton Network is not yet an open join. Mainnet is in an **invite-only phase**: validators need a sponsoring Super Validator, must supply a fixed egress IP, and onboarding secrets are one-time-use and expire after 48 hours [^s30]. The Tokenomics Committee at sync.global reviews applications with a stated turnaround of roughly two weeks [^s30]. Being approved as a **Super Validator** is heavier still — Super Validators run the BFT sequencing infrastructure, participate in governance through the Global Synchronizer Foundation, and earn a CC-denominated share of emissions for doing so [^s03][^s08]. The reward split is governance-controlled and will change: the current planned trajectory gives 62% of rewards to applications and 20% to Super Validators until mid-2029 [^s08]. Because Super Validator slots are finite and gated by GSF approval, they function more like permissioned-market-maker seats than an open PoS validator market _(interpretive)_ [^s03][^s30].

Commercial dependencies matter even for teams that never plan to run a Super Validator. Canton Enterprise bundles production-grade validator nodes, integration connectors, and infrastructure components under a non-open commercial license, with pricing by direct engagement rather than a public list [^s31]. Digital Asset closed a USD 135M strategic funding round in June 2025 led by DRW Venture Capital and Tradeweb, with BNP Paribas, Citadel Securities, DTCC, Goldman Sachs, and Circle Ventures participating [^s32] — evidence that the commercial layer is funded and active, but also that Digital Asset remains a single vendor concentrated at the heart of the stack _(interpretive)_ [^s31][^s32].

Interoperability is a two-part story. Inside Canton, atomic cross-domain transfer is native: a contract can migrate between synchronizers, and transactions that touch participants on multiple synchronizers compose atomically whenever the participants share a common synchronizer [^s33]. Outside Canton, the story is less mature. The Canton Network team explicitly frames external bridges (e.g. to Ethereum or Solana) as narrow-purpose connectors rather than the default integration path [^s34]. Circle's USDCx integration with Canton Network is one concrete recent example of the external-bridge direction, and independent industry coverage has treated this kind of integration as an emerging, not yet ubiquitous, capability _(interpretive)_ [^s34]. In short, interop works well _within_ Canton; interop with the rest of the crypto world is an ongoing roadmap item [^s33][^s34].

## 6. Limitations and Open Questions

**External validation is thin.** The arXiv paper on the Daml language is the only peer-reviewed artefact we found [^s35]. The Canton protocol whitepaper (s05) and Canton Network whitepaper (s06) are vendor-authored and were access-limited to direct PDF parsing in this survey — their textual claims are still supported indirectly through the public blog posts and docs [^s04][^s08], but an independent formal audit of sub-transaction privacy and of Canton's BFT properties at production scale is not something this report could locate.

**Quantitative performance claims are vendor-stated.** Throughput, latency, and fee-cost numbers for the Canton protocol originate from Digital Asset and partner blogs [^s07][^s08]. Broadridge DLR's monthly repo-volume press releases are independent and very large (US$368B/day in November 2025) [^s17][^s38], but those are trade-volume numbers from a private Daml deployment, not protocol TPS benchmarks for the public Canton Network. A reader who needs hard throughput numbers for the Global Synchronizer should treat the current numbers as snapshots rather than guarantees.

**Canton Coin tokenomics are young.** CC has been publicly circulating only since mid-2024 [^s02]; the reward split is scheduled to change over time [^s08]; and the Super Validator set is still gated by GSF approval rather than fully permissionless [^s30]. Any specific CC figure in this report should be read as a 2025-era snapshot, not an invariant of the protocol.

**Licensing is mixed.** Core Daml (s20) and Canton (s21) are Apache-2.0; Splice and the Global Synchronizer reference apps are Apache-2.0 [^s28][^s29]; but Canton Enterprise components are commercial [^s31]. Builders should map their target topology to the license each component actually ships under before assuming "open source" covers everything they need.

**Governance concentration remains open.** The Global Synchronizer Foundation is under Linux Foundation governance [^s03], but Digital Asset is both a founding member and the primary code author of Splice [^s28]. Whether GSF governance materially diffuses control over time is a question that can only be answered by watching who actually runs Super Validators, who authors protocol changes, and who sponsors new validators _(interpretive)_ [^s03][^s28][^s30].
